The most popular styles of architecture in England: from Tudors to High-tech

The architecture of modern Britain is a complex result of a mixture of different styles, including those that arose even before the formation of the state itself.

Of course, the basis of the architecture of the entire United Kingdom is the English style, but the styles of Ireland, Wales and Scotland also influenced it.

If we talk about classical British architecture, then there are three main historical periods with their own characteristic stylistics:

  • Tudor style (XVI century);
  • Georgian style (XVIII century);
  • Victorian style (XIX century).

When you hear about an English—style house somewhere, you can be almost sure that we are talking about a building from one of these three eras. In addition, Elizabethan architecture (16th — 17th centuries), English Baroque, English Art Deco, architecture of the 1930s and architecture of the postwar period can be considered separate trends.

Tudor style: attics and half-timbered
The style, called Tudor, was formed in the XVI century — it was named so in honor of the royal dynasty that ruled at that time. Tudor houses are distinguished by small windows, tall narrow doors and steep roofs. Another distinctive feature of the style is grouped chimneys of various shapes decorated with ceramics. A separate direction of the style was the half—timbered - the so-called white houses with an external wooden frame.

In fact, the Tudor style can be attributed to the late Gothic: here you can see the same elongated and narrow silhouettes, but with their own characteristics. The English architecture of this period was also influenced by the Baroque, so the Tudor buildings are distinguished by their ornate decor.



English Baroque
Baroque architecture was adopted by England in the XVII century and became an interpretation of classicism.

The first Baroque country houses appeared in the late 1690s. The most iconic buildings of this era were St. Paul's Cathedral and Greenwich Hospital, designed by Christopher Wren, as well as the Blenheim Palace by architect Nicholas Hawksmoor. Blenheim is the only building that did not belong to the royal family, while being called a palace. This is where Winston Churchill grew up.

Distinctive features of the style are complex, pretentious and even somewhat theatrical decorations, an abundance of domes characteristic of the Renaissance.

Georgian style: symmetry once again symmetry
Georgian—style houses are distinguished by restraint, but at the same time they look impressive - as a rule, we mean them when we mention English architecture in everyday dialogue. This style is characterized by red brick walls without unnecessary decorations, an equilibrium number of windows on the facades of houses, symmetrical foreground, average-height roof slopes, paired pipes. Only doorways are decorated: the doors of Georgian houses, as a rule, are decorated with panels and framed with pilasters. The buildings are surrounded by a plinth on all sides.
Victorian style: turrets and luxurious decor
The era of the reign of Queen Victoria turned out to be an extremely successful historical period for England: it was a time of rapid growth of industry and active accumulation of capital. A logical continuation of this growth is that at the same moment in society there is a demand for luxury, exquisite interiors and some "excesses" in architecture. In an attempt to understand what the Victorian style is, it is important to remember this detail: Queen Victoria ruled the country for a very long period, from 1837 to 1901. Therefore, there is nothing surprising in the fact that under the "Victorian architecture" there is a huge number of very different buildings, from neoclassical to Neo-Gothic.


The Victorian style is characterized by high decorative facades, complex asymmetrical shapes, steep roofs topped with turrets. Other characteristic features of the Victorian—era house are protruding bay windows, a veranda around one or two sides of the building and a pediment on the main facade. The walls of the house in the Victorian style, as a rule, are also decorated: it can be half-timbered, siding or masonry.

For the most part, the Victorian architectural style is Neo-Gothic: tired of Georgian restraint, the British public once again turned their attention to elegant decorative forms. The main difference between this architectural trend and Gothic was a greater bias in functionality: Gothic forms adapted to current needs, and practicality was no longer sacrificed for the sake of decor.
Architecture of London
The eclecticism of British architecture is especially evident on the streets of London. Here you can see Gothic cathedrals, Romanesque buildings of the XII-XV centuries, beautiful palaces of the XVI-XVIII centuries, the Tudor style already mentioned above, Georgian and Victorian buildings and, of course, ultra-modern office buildings.

At the same time, it can be noted that architecture of a particular style is inherent in different districts of the capital. For example, lovers of Victorian "gingerbread" houses and the famous terraced houses should go to the Islington area: it is there that you can find the very buildings that many of us imagine talking about English architecture.
